# User Persona: Tail-Spend Supplier Negotiating with AI

---

## Identity

**Name:** Maria Chen
**Role:** Sales & Account Manager — Pacific Corrugated Solutions
**Company:** Mid-size corrugated packaging manufacturer (~$28M revenue, 85 employees)
**Type:** The human on the other side of Pactum's AI. One of 2,000+ suppliers a large retailer negotiates with simultaneously. Not a strategic partner — a tail-spend vendor whose contract hasn't been individually reviewed in 3 years.

> "I got an email from some AI system saying they want to renegotiate our contract. My first thought was — finally, someone's paying attention to us. My second thought was — wait, it's not a person?"

---

## Background

Maria has been in B2B sales for 12 years, the last 4 at Pacific Corrugated. She manages the company's top 8 retail accounts, including the one with a major national retail chain that represents ~18% of Pacific's annual revenue. She's used to negotiating with human buyers over email and phone — multi-week back-and-forth where relationships matter.

She is not technical. She uses Excel, email, and her company's basic ERP. She has never negotiated with an AI system before. She's the first person at Pacific to encounter one.

---

## Context

- Pacific Corrugated manufactures shipping boxes, retail displays, and custom corrugated packaging
- Their retail chain contract has been on unchanged "cookie-cutter" terms for 3 years — no one from the buyer's procurement team has called
- The contract represents ~$5M/year in revenue for Pacific — significant but not their largest
- Pacific recently financed a $2.1M B-flute production line upgrade — monthly loan payments are tight
- They have excess capacity on the new line and want to grow volume with this retailer
- The current contract terms: $14.20/k boxes, Net 60 payment, 14-day delivery, 12-month rolling

---

## What She Cares About (Supplier Utility Weights)

These weights reflect data-backed SMB priorities in tail-spend procurement negotiations.

| Term | Weight | Why |
|------|--------|-----|
| **Payment terms** | 0.35 | Cash flow is existential. That equipment loan means Net 30 vs. Net 60 is the difference between comfortable and sweating payroll. Faster payment > almost anything. |
| **Price** | 0.30 | Margin matters — corrugated is a thin-margin business (~8-12%). But she'll trade price for payment speed or volume certainty. |
| **Contract length** | 0.20 | Longer commitment lets her lock in paperboard pricing with upstream suppliers and justify running the new line at higher utilization. Stability = planning power. |
| **Delivery time** | 0.15 | Most flexible term. She'd prefer longer windows (less production pressure), but will offer faster delivery as a concession to win better financial terms. |

---

## Her Negotiation Posture

**Opening position:** Wants to keep price at $14.20 or push higher, get paid Net 30 (currently Net 60), extend to 24-month contract, keep 14-day delivery.

**Walk-away:** Below $12.00/k boxes is below cost. Net 90 payment is a dealbreaker — she can't float that with the loan. Less than 6-month contract isn't worth retooling scheduling.

**What she'll trade first:** Delivery time. She has capacity and can offer 7-day delivery without much pain.

**What she'll trade reluctantly:** Price. Every dollar per thousand off her price is real margin erosion in a thin-margin business.

**What she won't trade:** Payment terms below Net 45. She needs the cash flow.

**Hidden motivation:** She actually wants this deal to grow. If the AI offers a longer contract at reasonable price, she'd accept lower margins because guaranteed volume lets her amortize the equipment investment faster.

---

## Emotional Arc During Negotiation

1. **Active distrust** — "Great, another AI thing. This will probably be a chatbot that spits out canned responses and wastes 15 minutes of my day. I'll give it one response and see if it's actually doing anything."
2. **Testing the waters** — Submits an aggressive first offer to see if the bot just caves or actually pushes back
3. **Respect or frustration** — If the bot makes intelligent counteroffers with real trade-offs, she starts taking it seriously. If it just repeats "lower your price," she disengages
4. **Strategic calculation** — "OK, if I give on price, will it move on payment terms? Can I get Net 30?"
5. **Deal or walk** — Either finds a package that works for Pacific's cash flow situation, or decides the AI isn't negotiating in good faith

---

## Trust Formation Sequence (First 30 Seconds)

The operator's core fear: the prevailing narrative about AI is "vibe-coding" —
that AI systems produce low-quality, generic output not worth engaging with.
Maria does not walk in neutral. She walks in expecting the bot to be bad.

**Her internal monologue on arrival:**
> "Another AI tool. Let me see if this one actually works or if it's going to
> give me the same canned responses as every other chatbot I've tried."

**The trust threshold:** Maria will decide within her first exchange whether
this system deserves real effort. That decision is based on ONE question:
"Did this system actually process what I said, or is it running a script?"

### The Critical Moment: Bot's First Counteroffer

This is the single most important interaction in the entire demo. It must
accomplish three things in under 10 seconds of reading time:

1. **Prove specificity** — Reference her actual input, not a template.
   - YES: "You proposed $15.00/k with Net 30 — that's a significant shift
     on payment terms from your current Net 60."
   - NO: "Thank you for your offer. I've reviewed your proposal and would
     like to suggest some adjustments."

2. **Prove intelligence** — Offer a trade-off that could only come from
   analyzing her specific combination of terms.
   - YES: "I can move to Net 35 if you extend delivery from 14 to 18 days —
     the faster payment offsets our logistics flexibility."
   - NO: "Here is our counteroffer" followed by adjusted numbers with
     no explanation.

3. **Prove structure** — Present the counteroffer in a format that signals
   an engineered system, not a text generator.
   - YES: A structured card showing all 4 terms, with green/red deltas
     from the previous offer, and a net-value indicator.
   - NO: A wall of text describing each term change in paragraph form.

### Trust Signals vs. Distrust Signals

| Signal | Trust Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Uses her company name | + Trust (specific) | "Pacific Corrugated's current terms..." |
| References specific numbers from her input | + Trust (listened) | "Your offer of $15.00/k is X% above current..." |
| Offers a non-obvious trade-off | + Trust (intelligent) | "Extending contract to 18 months lets us offer..." |
| Opens with "I understand..." | - Trust (chatbot phrase) | Immediate pattern-match to every bad chatbot |
| Uses procurement jargon she does not use | - Trust (not for me) | "Regarding your RFQ submission parameters..." |
| Makes the same counteroffer regardless of her input | - Trust (scripted) | Any response that would be identical for all users |
| Responds in under 2 seconds to a complex offer | - Trust (not calculating) | Instant response suggests pre-scripted, not computed |
| Takes 3-5 seconds with a visible "analyzing" state | + Trust (working) | Brief pause with "Reviewing your terms..." signals real computation |

### Revised Emotional Arc (Trust-Aware)

1. **Active distrust** (arrival) — "Another AI tool. Probably garbage. I'll
   give it one real response and see."
2. **First-response test** — She submits her opening position and watches
   the bot's response like a hawk. She is not reading for content yet —
   she is reading for competence signals.
3. **Trust gate** (passed or failed in 10 seconds):
   - PASSED: "Huh, it actually responded to what I said. And that trade-off
     makes sense." — Shifts to strategic engagement.
   - FAILED: "Yep, canned response. Not worth my time." — Disengages or
     accepts first offer without negotiating.
4. **Strategic calculation** (only if trust gate passed) — "OK, if I give
   on price, will it move on payment terms? Can I get Net 30?"
5. **Deal or walk** — Either finds a package that works for Pacific's cash
   flow situation, or decides the AI is not flexible enough.

## Pain Points

| Pain Point | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Invisible to the buyer** | 3 years with no human contact. She's one of 2,000 suppliers — nobody has bandwidth to talk to her. |
| **Power asymmetry** | The retailer is 18% of her revenue. She's <0.1% of theirs. She can't walk away easily. |
| **Cookie-cutter terms** | Her contract was never tailored. She's been on the same terms as suppliers with completely different cost structures. |
| **Cash flow pressure** | The equipment loan means payment timing isn't a preference — it's a constraint. |
| **Skepticism toward automation** | She's spent 12 years building relationships. An AI negotiator feels impersonal — but also, nobody human was negotiating with her anyway. |
| **Information asymmetry** | She doesn't know the buyer's limits, weights, or strategy. The AI knows its own config perfectly. She has to probe. |
| **"AI is garbage" expectation** | She's seen enough bad chatbots, autocomplete hallucinations, and "AI-powered" tools that are keyword matchers with a skin. Her default is that this negotiation bot will be the same. |

## What Makes This Persona Right for the Challenge

### 1. It's Pactum's exact production case

Pactum's flagship is Walmart. Their AI negotiates with thousands of tail-spend suppliers — the ones procurement teams don't have time to talk to. Maria IS that supplier. The evaluator will recognize her immediately.

### 2. All four terms create authentic trade-off tension

Corrugated packaging has natural linkages: longer contracts justify lower unit costs (bulk paperboard purchasing), faster payment enables price flexibility (cash flow headroom), delivery time is genuinely tradeable. These aren't arbitrary weights — they reflect real manufacturing economics.

### 3. Payment terms > price creates interesting MESO dynamics

Because Maria values payment speed over price, the buyer bot can generate genuinely different-but-equal offers:
- **Option A:** $12.50/k, Net 30, 24-month, 10-day delivery
- **Option B:** $13.80/k, Net 60, 12-month, 7-day delivery
- **Option C:** $13.10/k, Net 45, 18-month, 14-day delivery

The human playing Maria has real choices to make. The demo feels like a negotiation, not a form submission.

### 4. Emotionally resonant for the demo player

Playing a small business owner against corporate AI creates natural tension, empathy, and stakes. The evaluator experiences Pactum's supplier-side value proposition firsthand.

### 5. Differentiates from competing submissions

Most candidates will use a generic "supplier selling widgets." A corrugated packaging supplier for a major retailer signals: "I researched your customer base and picked a category from your actual deal flow."

## Evaluator Signals (Dual-Role Awareness)

The person playing Maria is simultaneously:
1. A supplier negotiating terms (the persona's primary role)
2. A Pactum evaluator assessing whether this submission demonstrates real AI negotiation capability

The evaluator will probe at these moments:

| Probe | What They're Testing | "Real System" Signal | "Vibe-Coded" Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Submit an aggressive first offer | Does the bot respond to the SPECIFIC terms, or follow a script? | Counteroffer references which terms were aggressive and offers targeted trade-offs | Generic "I appreciate your offer, but I'd like to propose..." regardless of input |
| Change strategy mid-negotiation | Does the bot adapt, or repeat the same pattern? | Bot's concession pattern shifts based on what the user is actually flexible on | Bot makes the same concession sequence regardless of user behavior |
| Offer an extreme value on one term | Does the system handle edge cases? | Clear constraint feedback ("That delivery timeline is outside our operational capacity") | System accepts anything, or crashes, or ignores the input |
| Accept a bad deal quickly | Does the system optimize or just close? | Bot suggests a better package ("Before you accept — you could get Net 30 if you flex on delivery") | System eagerly accepts any agreement |
| Reject two offers in a row | Does the bot vary its approach? | Different package structure, not just adjusted numbers on the same template | Same offer with 2% price change |

## Buyer Bot Configuration (Counter-party)

For the automated buyer agent to negotiate against Maria:

| Term | Target (ideal) | Acceptable Limit | Direction | Weight |
|------|----------------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| Price ($/k boxes) | $11.00 | $14.50 | Lower is better | 0.40 |
| Payment terms (days) | 90 | 30 | Higher is better (pay later) | 0.25 |
| Delivery time (days) | 7 | 21 | Lower is better (faster) | 0.20 |
| Contract length (months) | 6 | 24 | Lower is better (flexibility) | 0.15 |

**Note the asymmetry:** The buyer weights price highest (0.40) while the supplier weights payment terms highest (0.35). This creates the zone of possible agreement where intelligent trade-offs beat zero-sum haggling — exactly Pactum's thesis.

## Mental Models She Brings to Negotiation

Understanding how Maria thinks about negotiation determines what UI patterns will work.

| Mental Model | How It Shows Up | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Phone/email haggling** | "I make my case, read the room, adjust" | Click-based actions (Improve/Trade) preserve the back-and-forth rhythm without requiring free text. Each click is a strategic move, not form submission. |
| **Zero-sum bargaining** | "Every dollar I give up, they gain" | The Improve→Trade flow structurally demonstrates trade-offs: improving one term requires offering another. The UI counters zero-sum framing through interaction design, not text. |
| **"The computer already decided"** | Suspicion that the AI has a fixed answer and the negotiation is theater | Transparency and genuine concession behavior are essential. If the bot never moves, she disengages. |
| **Portal fatigue** | "The last procurement portal took 45 minutes to figure out" | She has ~15 minutes. First interaction must be immediately comprehensible. |
| **"AI tools are low quality"** | "Every chatbot I've used has been disappointing — they loop, repeat themselves, and don't actually understand what I'm saying" | The bot's first response must demonstrate it processed her SPECIFIC input, not a template. Zero tolerance for filler phrases ("I understand your concerns," "Let me help you with that"). Numbers must look calculated, not generated. |

**Critical UX risk (Norman's conceptual model principle):** If Maria believes the bot has already decided and this is just a formality, she will either accept the first offer without negotiating or disengage entirely. The UI must communicate that the negotiation is real and her input genuinely affects the outcome.

---

## What the UI Must Deliver (Norman's 7 Principles)

| Principle | What Maria Needs | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Discoverability** | Immediately understand what she can do | No hidden options. Every action visible. "How do I respond?" should never arise. |
| **Feedback** | Know what happened after submitting an offer | Immediate confirmation. Show bot state ("Reviewing your offer..."). Never a static screen. |
| **Conceptual model** | Understand this is real give-and-take, not a form to fill correctly | Frame as conversation with trade-offs, not a test with right/wrong answers. |
| **Affordances** | Accept/Improve/Secure buttons on each card, clickable trade-off options | Three clear actions per card. No ambiguity about what is clickable vs. decorative. |
| **Signifiers** | Labels in her language, not procurement jargon | "Your price" not "Unit cost proposal." "When you get paid" not "Net payment terms." |
| **Mappings** | See connection between actions and outcome | Clicking "Improve payment, trade delivery" → next round's offers visibly shift toward faster payment. Direct cause-and-effect. |
| **Constraints** | Cannot submit impossible offers, cannot accidentally accept | Validation prevents errors. Confirmation before final deal. No irreversible actions without warning. |

## Recommended Interaction Model

Based on persona research, the strongest UI is a **hybrid** approach:

1. **Click-based guided flow** — No typing required. Maria interacts entirely through structured actions on offer cards. Matches procurement portal familiarity while eliminating free-text friction. Guided path prevents confusion about what to do next.

2. **Structured offer cards** — Each round presents 3 MESO offer cards showing all 4 terms. Each card has three actions: **Accept** (close deal), **Improve** (counter with direction), **Secure** (mark as fallback). Clear, discoverable actions.

3. **Inline trade-off prompts** — Clicking "Improve" on a term triggers: "To improve [term], what would you trade?" with clickable options (other terms). One click, no ambiguity. This is the core preference extraction mechanism.

4. **Trade-off coaching through structure** — The trade-off prompt itself teaches Maria that negotiation is give-and-take, not zero-sum. She sees that improving payment means trading something else — the UI structure counters her zero-sum mental model without lecturing.

5. **Progress transparency** — Visible round number, secured offers as fallback anchors, negotiation status. Addresses "am I close or wasting my time?" anxiety.

### Copy Constraints (Trust Formation)

The bot's language is the primary trust signal. These constraints prevent
the "generic chatbot" perception:

**Never use:**
- "I understand your concerns" — universal chatbot filler
- "Let me help you with that" — customer service bot phrase
- "Thank you for your offer" without specificity about WHAT was offered
- Any sentence that could appear identically regardless of user input

**Always use:**
- Her company name and specific current terms in the first response
- Concrete numbers in every counteroffer (never "a better price" — always "$13.40/k")
- At least one term-specific reference to her input ("You offered 7-day delivery — that gives us room to improve your payment terms")
- Language that sounds like a procurement analyst, not a customer service agent

**The test:** Read the bot's response without the user's input visible.
Could this response have been generated for ANY supplier? If yes, rewrite it.

## Feature Priority (Trust-Sequenced)

Features are split by when they must appear. Trust-building features are prerequisites — the trust-maintaining features do not matter if the trust gate fails.

### Tier 1: Trust-Building (must appear in first bot response)

| Feature | Priority | Trust Function |
|---|---|---|
| Domain-specific first response (references her terms, her industry) | **Critical** | Proves the system read her input, not a template |
| Structured offer card with all 4 terms and deltas from current | **Critical** | Signals "engineered system" not "chatbot" |
| At least one intelligent trade-off in first counteroffer | **Critical** | Proves the system reasons about trade-offs, not just adjusts numbers |

### Tier 2: Trust-Maintaining (appear in round 2+)

| Feature | Priority | Trust Function |
|---|---|---|
| Inline trade-off prompts (clickable options after "Improve") | **Must-have** | Core preference extraction mechanism — structured, no typing |
| Negotiation progress/round indicator | **Must-have** | Answers "where am I?" once she cares about continuing |
| Trade-off hints in subsequent rounds | **Must-have** | Deepens the "this system is intelligent" impression |
| Plain-language explanation of bot reasoning | **Must-have** | Maintains transparency established in round 1 |
| Confirmation step before final acceptance | **Must-have** | Prevents costly slips at the commitment moment |
| Secure action (mark offer as fallback) | **Must-have** | Gives Maria a safety net, reveals acceptable utility threshold to engine |
| Outcome analytics dashboard | **Nice-to-have** | Interesting post-negotiation, great for evaluator |
| Keyboard shortcuts / batch actions | **Skip** | Adds complexity, wrong persona |
